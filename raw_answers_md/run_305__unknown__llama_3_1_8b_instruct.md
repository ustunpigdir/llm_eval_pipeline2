## Step 1: State the one-loop (Schwinger) result for $a_e$ in terms of $\alpha$.
The one-loop QED correction to the electron's magnetic moment is given by the Schwinger result:
\[a_e = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\]

## Step 2: Evaluate $a_e \times 10^3$.
Given $\alpha = 1/137.036$, we can substitute this value into the expression for $a_e$:
\[a_e \times 10^3 = \frac{1/137.036}{2\pi} \times 10^3\]
\[a_e \times 10^3 = \frac{10^3}{2\pi \times 137.036}\]
\[a_e \times 10^3 \approx \frac{10^3}{863.59}\]
\[a_e \times 10^3 \approx 1.1597\]

## Step 3: Evaluate the $g$-factor to 6 decimal places.
The $g$-factor is given by:
\[g = 2(1 + a_e)\]
Substituting the value of $a_e$ from Step 2:
\[g = 2(1 + 1.1597 \times 10^{-3})\]
\[g \approx 2.002319\]

## Step 4: Explain physically what the anomaly measures (tree-level Dirac vs loop corrections), and why the SAME leading coefficient applies to the muon.
The anomaly $a_e$ measures the difference between the observed magnetic moment of the electron and the Dirac value, which is a measure of the electron's intrinsic spin. The loop corrections, which are the one-loop QED corrections, contribute to this difference. The same leading coefficient applies to the muon because the muon's magnetic moment is also affected by the same loop corrections, which are proportional to the fine-structure constant $\alpha$. The muon's larger mass does not affect the leading coefficient, which is determined by the loop corrections.
