**1. Unruh temperature formula and physical statement:**

The Unruh temperature $T_U$ for a uniformly accelerated observer in the Minkowski vacuum is given by:

\[T_U = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}\]

where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, $a$ is the proper acceleration, $c$ is the speed of light, and $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant. The accelerated detector sees a thermal bath of particles with this temperature, known as the Unruh effect.

**2. Evaluation of $T_U$ for $a = 10^{20}$ m/s$^2$:**

\[T_U = \frac{\hbar \times 10^{20} \text{ m/s}^2}{2\pi c k_B}\]

Using the values $\hbar = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s, $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s, and $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K:

\[T_U = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 10^{20}}{2\pi \times 3.00 \times 10^8 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23}}\]

\[T_U = \frac{6.63 \times 10^4}{2\pi \times 3.90 \times 10^5}\]

\[T_U = \frac{6.63 \times 10^4}{2.47 \times 10^5}\]

\[T_U = 26.8 \text{ K}\]

**3. Proper acceleration for $T_U = 1.00$ K:**

\[1.00 = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}\]

\[a = \frac{1.00 \times 2\pi c k_B}{\hbar}\]

\[a = \frac{2\pi \times 3.00 \times 10^8 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23}}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}\]

\[a = \frac{2.47 \times 10^5}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}\]

\[a = 3.72 \times 10^{28} \text{ m/s}^2\]

Converting to units of $10^{20}$ m/s$^2$:

\[a = 3.72 \times 10^{8}\]

**4. Relation to Hawking radiation and everyday accelerations:**

The equivalence principle states that an observer in free fall experiences no physical effects. Thus, an observer accelerating through flat spacetime is equivalent to an observer at rest near a black hole's event horizon. This is why the accelerated detector sees thermal radiation (Unruh effect) similar to the blackbody radiation (Hawking radiation) emitted by black holes.

Everyday accelerations are far too small to produce observable Unruh temperatures. For instance, an astronaut in low Earth orbit experiences an acceleration of about $9.0 \times 10^{-3}$ m/s$^2$, which would result in an Unruh temperature of approximately $1.5 \times 10^{-16}$ K, far below any measurable temperature.
