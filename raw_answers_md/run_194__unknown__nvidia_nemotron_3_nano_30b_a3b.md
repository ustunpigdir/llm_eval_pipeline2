The leading‑order (quadrupole) frequency evolution for a circular inspiral is  

\[
\dot f_{GW}= \frac{96}{5}\,\pi^{8/3}\,\mathcal{M}^{5/3}\,f_{GW}^{11/3}\,,
\]

so that  

\[
\mathcal{M}= \left(\frac{5}{96}\,\frac{\dot f_{GW}}{\pi^{8/3}\,f_{GW}^{11/3}}\right)^{3/5}.
\]

Insert the measured values  

\[
f_{GW}=35.0\ {\rm Hz},\qquad 
\dot f_{GW}=68.678\ {\rm Hz\,s^{-1}} .
\]

\[
\pi^{8/3}\simeq 21.176,\qquad 
f_{GW}^{11/3}=35^{11/3}\simeq 4.58\times10^{5},
\]

\[
\pi^{8/3}f_{GW}^{11/3}\simeq 9.70\times10^{6},
\qquad 
\frac{\dot f_{GW}}{\pi^{8/3}f_{GW}^{11/3}}\simeq 7.08\times10^{-6},
\]

\[
\frac{5}{96}\times\frac{\dot f_{GW}}{\pi^{8/3}f_{GW}^{11/3}}
\simeq 3.69\times10^{-7},
\]

\[
\mathcal{M}_{\rm (s)}\;=\;(3.69\times10^{-7})^{3/5}
\simeq 1.38\times10^{-4}\ {\rm s}.
\]

Convert the chirp‑mass expressed in seconds to solar‑mass units using  

\[
\frac{G M_\odot}{c^{3}} = 4.925\times10^{-6}\ {\rm s},
\]

\[
\mathcal{M}_{\rm MSUN}= \frac{1.38\times10^{-4}}{4.925\times10^{-6}}
\simeq 27.95\ M_\odot .
\]

---

**Equal‑mass assumption**  

For \(m_1=m_2=m\),

\[
\mathcal{M}= \frac{m}{2^{1/5}}\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
m = \mathcal{M}\,2^{1/5}
      \simeq 27.95 \times 1.1487
      \simeq 32.11\ M_\odot .
\]

Hence  

\[
\text{total mass}=2m \simeq 64.22\ M_\odot .
\]

---

**Why the leading‑order waveform measures the chirp mass**  

At Newtonian (quadrupole) order the phase evolution – and therefore the observed chirp in frequency – depends only on the combination  

\[
\mathcal{M}= \frac{(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}}{(m_1+m_2)^{1/5}},
\]

which is the chirp mass.  The total mass enters only through higher‑order PN corrections (e.g. the symmetric‑mass‑ratio \(\eta\)).  Consequently, the measured \(\dot f_{GW}\) at a given \(f_{GW}\) fixes \(\mathcal{M}\) uniquely, while the individual masses remain degenerate without additional information (e.g. amplitude or higher‑order PN terms).

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{chirp\_mass\_msun} &= 27.95 \\
\mathrm{each\_mass\_msun} &= 32.11 \\
\mathrm{total\_mass\_msun} &= 64.22
\end{aligned}
\]
