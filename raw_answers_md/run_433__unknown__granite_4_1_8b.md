**Step‑by‑step reasoning**

1. **Entanglement of the Werner state**  
   The Werner state is  
   \[
   \rho = p\,|\Psi^{-}\rangle\langle\Psi^{-}| + (1-p)\,\frac{\mathbb{I}}{4},
   \]
   where \(|\Psi^{-}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)\).  
   For two‑qubit states the Peres–Horodecki (PPT) criterion is both necessary and sufficient: a state is separable iff its partial transpose has non‑negative eigenvalues.  

   The singlet \(|\Psi^{-}\rangle\langle\Psi^{-}|\) is PPT‑negative (its partial transpose has a negative eigenvalue). Adding a mixture of the maximally mixed state \(\mathbb{I}/4\) (which is PPT‑positive) can only increase the eigenvalues of the partial transpose.  

   Computing the eigenvalues of \(\rho^{T_B}\) (partial transpose on the second qubit) gives  
   \[
   \lambda_{\pm}(p)=\frac{1}{2}\Bigl[ \frac{1}{2} \pm \sqrt{\frac{1}{4} - 4p(1-p)} \Bigr].
   \]
   The most negative eigenvalue is \(\lambda_{-}(p)\). The state is entangled when \(\lambda_{-}(p)<0\):
   \[
   \frac{1}{2} - \sqrt{\frac{1}{4} - 4p(1-p)} < 0
   \;\Longrightarrow\;
   \sqrt{\frac{1}{4} - 4p(1-p)} > \frac{1}{2}
   \;\Longrightarrow\;
   \frac{1}{4} - 4p(1-p) < \frac{1}{4}
   \;\Longrightarrow\;
   -4p(1-p) < 0
   \;\Longrightarrow\;
   p(1-p) > 0.
   \]
   Since \(0\le p\le1\), the inequality reduces to \(p> \tfrac13\).  
   **Entanglement threshold:** \(p_{\text{ent}} = \frac13\).

2. **CHSH violation of the Werner state**  
   The maximal quantum CHSH value attainable with a given state is  
   \[
   S_{\max}(p)=2\sqrt{2}\,p.
   \]
   A non‑local (CHSH‑violating) outcome requires \(S>2\). Setting  
   \[
   2\sqrt{2}\,p > 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sqrt{2}\,p > 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; p > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.
   \]
   **CHSH threshold:** \(p_{\text{CHSH}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\).

3. **Values at \(p = 0.5\)**  

   - **Concurrence**  
     The concurrence for a Werner state is known to be  
     \[
     C(p)=\max\!\Bigl(0,\frac{2p-1}{2}\Bigr).
     \]
     Substituting \(p=0.5\):  
     \[
     C(0.5)=\max\!\Bigl(0,\frac{2\cdot0.5-1}{2}\Bigr)=\max(0,0)=0.
     \]  

   - **Maximal CHSH value**  
     \[
     S_{\max}(0.5)=2\sqrt{2}\times0.5 = \sqrt{2}\approx1.414.
     \]  
     Since \(\sqrt{2}<2\), the state does **not** violate CHSH at this point.

4. **Why entanglement and Bell nonlocality differ**  
   The Werner state with \(p=0.5\) is **entangled** (it is PPT‑negative, i.e., non‑separable) but **fails to violate the CHSH inequality** because its maximal CHSH value is \(\sqrt{2}<2\). This demonstrates that:

   - **Entanglement** is a resource that can be used in many quantum protocols (e.g., teleportation, superdense coding) even when the state is mixed and may not be detectable by local hidden‑variable models.
   - **Bell nonlocality (CHSH violation)** is a stricter condition: it requires that the correlations generated by the state cannot be reproduced by any local hidden‑variable theory.  

   Werner’s 1989 paper showed that there exist entangled states (the Werner family) that are “non‑signalling” in the sense that they never produce a CHSH violation, highlighting that **entanglement is not equivalent to Bell nonlocality**. The distinction lies in the amount of *strong* quantum correlations (those capable of violating CHSH) versus the more general presence of quantum correlations (entanglement) that may be too weak or mixed to yield observable nonlocal effects.
