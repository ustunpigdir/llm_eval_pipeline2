**1. Lorentz factor at \(v=0.8c\)**  

\[
\beta = \frac{v}{c}=0.8,\qquad 
\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^{2}}}
        = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.8^{2}}}
        = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}}
        = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}}
        = \frac{1}{0.6}
        = \frac{5}{3}\approx1.6667 .
\]

**2. Lab‑frame separation after coasting**  

Both rockets share the same velocity function \(v(t)\) at every lab‑frame instant, so their world‑lines remain parallel with a constant offset equal to the initial separation. Hence the distance measured in the original lab frame stays  

\[
\Delta x_{\text{lab}} = L_{0}=100\ \text{m}.
\]

**3. Proper separation in the rockets’ final rest frame**  

The lab measurement is a coordinate length. The proper length (the distance measured simultaneously in the rockets’ common rest frame) is larger by the factor \(\gamma\):

\[
L_{\text{proper}} = \gamma\,L_{\text{lab}}
                    = \frac{5}{3}\times100\ \text{m}
                    = \frac{500}{3}\ \text{m}
                    \approx 166.67\ \text{m}.
\]

**4. Does the string break?**  

Yes. The proper separation in the final rest frame (\(\approx166.7\) m) exceeds the string’s rest length (100 m). In the rockets’ rest frame the string would have to be stretched beyond its material limit, producing an unbounded tension that cannot be sustained; therefore the string breaks.

**5. Why the student’s argument is wrong**  

The student treats the lab separation as a “length” that undergoes length contraction to \(L_{0}/\gamma\). This is incorrect because the separation between two world‑lines is not a physical object with a rest frame; it is a coordinate distance measured in the lab frame. Length contraction applies to a rod’s proper length measured in its own rest frame, not to a coordinate separation between two moving objects. Consequently, the proper separation is *larger*, not smaller, than the lab separation, and the string does not go slack.

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{gamma\_factor} &= \frac{5}{3} \\
\mathrm{lab\_separation\_m} &= 100 \\
\mathrm{proper\_separation\_m} &= \frac{500}{3}
\end{aligned}
}
\]
