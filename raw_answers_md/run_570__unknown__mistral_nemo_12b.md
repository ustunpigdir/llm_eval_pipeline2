**1. Derivation of lab-frame speed of light using relativistic velocity addition**

Let's consider the water flowing at velocity $V$ in the lab frame. In the water's rest frame, the phase velocity of light is $c/n$. To find the lab-frame speed of light, we use the relativistic velocity addition formula:

\[u = \frac{v + V}{1 + \frac{vV}{c^2}}\]

where $u$ is the lab-frame speed of light, $v$ is the phase velocity in the water's rest frame ($v = c/n$), and $V$ is the water's velocity in the lab frame. Expanding this to first order in $V/c$:

\[u = \frac{c/n + V}{1 + \frac{cV/n}{c^2}} \approx c/n + \frac{nV}{c}\]

So, the lab-frame speed of light is $u \approx c/n + fV$, where the Fresnel drag coefficient $f$ is:

\[f = \frac{n}{c}\]

**2. Evaluation of the drag coefficient**

For $n = 1.333$:

\[f = \frac{1.333}{c} \approx 4.44 \times 10^{-7}\]

**3. Evaluation of the increase in the light's lab speed**

The increase in the light's lab speed is:

\[\Delta v = fV = (4.44 \times 10^{-7})(5.00) = 2.22 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m/s}\]

**4. Why the Galilean result is wrong and Fizeau's experiment**

The Galilean result $\Delta v = V$ is wrong because it assumes that velocities simply add, which is not the case in special relativity. Fizeau's experiment actually measured the time it takes for light to travel through a moving medium, which is related to the phase velocity, not the group velocity. The experiment confirmed the relativistic prediction that light experiences a drag effect when moving through a moving medium.
