### 1. Horizon Radius Calculation

The de Sitter horizon radius $r$ is defined as $r = c/H$.
Given:
-   $H = 70.0 \text{ km/s/Mpc}$
-   $c \approx 299,792.458 \text{ km/s}$
-   $1 \text{ Mpc} \approx 3.08567758 \times 10^{19} \text{ km}$

First, calculate $r$ in Mpc:
$$ r = \frac{299,792.458 \text{ km/s}}{70.0 \text{ km/s/Mpc}} \approx 4,282.75 \text{ Mpc} $$

Convert to Gpc ($1 \text{ Gpc} = 1,000 \text{ Mpc}$):
$$ r \approx 4.28275 \text{ Gpc} $$
Rounding to three significant figures (consistent with $H=70.0$):
$$ \mathrm{horizon\_gpc} \approx 4.28 $$

### 2. Gibbons-Hawking Temperature Calculation

The Gibbons-Hawking temperature is given by $T = \frac{\hbar H}{2\pi k_B}$.
We need $H$ in SI units ($\text{s}^{-1}$):
$$ H = \frac{70.0 \text{ km/s}}{3.08567758 \times 10^{19} \text{ km}} \approx 2.26854 \times 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1} $$

Constants:
-   $\hbar \approx 1.0545718 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
-   $k_B \approx 1.380649 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}$

Substitute into the temperature formula:
$$ T = \frac{(1.0545718 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}) \times (2.26854 \times 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1})}{2\pi \times (1.380649 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K})} $$
$$ T = \frac{2.3922 \times 10^{-52} \text{ J}}{8.6732 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}} $$
$$ T \approx 2.7581 \times 10^{-30} \text{ K} $$

The problem asks for the value in units of $10^{-30}$ K:
$$ \mathrm{gh\_temp\_e30\_K} \approx 2.76 $$

### 3. E-folds Calculation

We need to find the number of e-folds $N$ such that the CMB temperature $T_{CMB}$ cools from $2.725$ K to $T_{GH}$.
Assuming $T \propto a^{-1}$, the number of e-folds is given by the ratio of scale factors, which corresponds to the inverse ratio of temperatures:
$$ N = \ln\left(\frac{T_{initial}}{T_{final}}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{2.725}{T_{GH}}\right) $$

Using $T_{GH} \approx 2.7581 \times 10^{-30} \text{ K}$:
$$ \frac{2.725}{2.7581 \times 10^{-30}} \approx 9.880 \times 10^{29} $$
$$ N = \ln(9.880 \times 10^{29}) = \ln(9.880) + 29 \ln(10) $$
$$ N \approx 2.290 + 29(2.3026) \approx 2.290 + 66.775 \approx 69.065 $$

Rounding to three significant figures:
$$ \mathrm{efolds\_to\_gh} \approx 69.1 $$

### 4. Physical Interpretation

An observer in pure de Sitter space is surrounded by a cosmological event horizon at $r = c/H$. Due to the quantum field theoretic effects in curved spacetime, this observer detects a thermal bath of particles (Gibbons-Hawking radiation) with temperature $T_{GH}$.

**Analogy to Hawking and Unruh Effects:**
-   **Hawking Effect:** A black hole has an event horizon. An observer outside the horizon detects thermal radiation emitted by the black hole. The de Sitter horizon is analogous to a black hole horizon in that it is a causal boundary beyond which information cannot reach the observer.
-   **Unruh Effect:** An accelerating observer in flat spacetime detects a thermal bath (Unruh radiation) proportional to their acceleration. In de Sitter space, the observer is effectively "accelerating" relative to the static patch coordinates due to the expansion of the universe, leading to a similar thermal perception.
-   **de Sitter:** The cosmological horizon acts as the source of the thermal noise. Unlike the black hole case (where the horizon area decreases with evaporation), the de Sitter horizon area is constant in the pure de Sitter limit, implying a stable thermal equilibrium temperature.
