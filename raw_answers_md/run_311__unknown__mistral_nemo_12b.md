**1. One-loop (Schwinger) result for \(a_e\):**

The one-loop QED correction to the electron's magnetic moment is given by the Schwinger formula:

\[a_e = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\]

where \(\alpha\) is the fine structure constant. The anomaly \(a_e\) is defined as \(a_e = \frac{g-2}{2}\), where \(g\) is the \(g\)-factor.

**2. Evaluation of \(a_e \times 10^3\):**

Given \(\alpha = \frac{1}{137.036}\), we have:

\[a_e = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 137.036} \approx 1.166 \times 10^{-3}\]

So, \(a_e \times 10^3 \approx 1.166\).

**3. Evaluation of the \(g\)-factor:**

The \(g\)-factor is given by:

\[g = 2(1 + a_e) = 2\left(1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\right) \approx 2\left(1 + \frac{1}{2\pi \times 137.036}\right) \approx 2.00232\]

So, the \(g\)-factor to 6 decimal places is approximately 2.002320.

**4. Physical interpretation and muon anomaly:**

The anomaly \(a_e\) measures the difference between the tree-level Dirac magnetic moment (which is exactly 1 for a free electron) and the loop-corrected magnetic moment. The loop corrections account for the effects of virtual photons and electron-positron pairs, which cause the magnetic moment to deviate from its Dirac value.

The same leading coefficient applies to the muon because the dominant contribution to the anomaly comes from the same QED processes (vacuum polarization and vertex correction) that affect the electron. The difference in the numerical value of the anomaly between the electron and muon is due to their different masses and is a higher-order effect.
