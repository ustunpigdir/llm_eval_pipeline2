Okay, let's compute the one-loop QED anomalous magnetic moment of the electron.

**1. State the one-loop (Schwinger) result for *a<sub>e</sub>* in terms of α.**

The one-loop QED anomalous magnetic moment, as calculated by Schwinger (1948), is given by:

$$a_e = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}$$

where α is the fine-structure constant.

**2. Evaluate *a<sub>e</sub>* × 10<sup>3</sup>.**

We are given α = 1/137.036.  Therefore:

$$a_e = \frac{1/137.036}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 137.036} \approx \frac{1}{863.875} \approx 0.00116006$$

Multiplying by 10<sup>3</sup>:

$$a_e \times 10^3 \approx 0.00116006 \times 10^3 = 1.16006$$

**3. Evaluate the *g*-factor to 6 decimal places.**

The *g*-factor is related to the anomaly by:

$$g = 2(1 + a_e)$$

Substituting the value of *a<sub>e</sub>*:

$$g = 2(1 + 0.00116006) = 2(1.00116006) = 2.00232012$$

Rounding to 6 decimal places:

$$g \approx 2.002320$$

**4. Explain physically what the anomaly measures (tree-level Dirac vs loop corrections), and why the SAME leading coefficient applies to the muon.**

*Physically, the anomaly measures the deviation of the electron's magnetic moment from the Dirac prediction.*

*Tree-level Dirac Prediction:*  The Dirac equation predicts a magnetic moment of exactly *g* = 2 for a spin-1/2 particle like the electron. This is a consequence of the fundamental relationship between spin and magnetic moment.

*Loop Corrections:* Quantum Electrodynamics (QED) introduces loop corrections to this prediction. These corrections arise from the electron virtually emitting and reabsorbing photons.  The one-loop calculation, as we've done here, gives a small correction to the magnetic moment. This correction is *not* zero, meaning the electron's magnetic moment is slightly different from the Dirac prediction.  The anomaly, *a<sub>e</sub>*, quantifies this difference.

*Why the same leading coefficient for the muon:* The leading coefficient of the anomalous magnetic moment, α/(2π), is a consequence of the fundamental symmetries of QED.  The muon, like the electron, is a charged fermion that interacts with photons. The same QED interaction governs both particles. The leading-order correction to the anomalous magnetic moment arises from the same Feynman diagrams (electron loops emitting and absorbing photons).  Therefore, the leading-order correction is proportional to α/(2π) for both the electron and the muon. The difference in their anomalous magnetic moments arises from the different values of α (which depend on the mass of the particle, through the running coupling constant), but the *form* of the correction is the same.  Higher-order corrections, involving heavier particles, will differ between the electron and the muon due to their different masses.
