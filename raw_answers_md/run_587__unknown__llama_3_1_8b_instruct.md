To solve this problem, we will use the exact Rindler relations for hyperbolic motion. The Rindler relations are given by:

**Task 2: After $\tau = 1.00$ proper year, evaluate the elapsed lab time in years.**

We will substitute $\tau = 1.00$ yr into the expression for $t(\tau)$:

$$t(1.00) = \frac{1}{a} \left( \cosh(a \cdot 1.00) - 1 \right)$$

Given $a = 9.8$ m/s$^2$ and $1$ yr $= 3.15576 \times 10^7$ s, we can calculate $a$ in units of s$^{-1}$:

$$a = \frac{9.8 \text{ m/s}^2}{3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s/yr}} = 3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}$$

Now we can substitute $a$ into the expression for $t(1.00)$:

$$t(1.00) = \frac{1}{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}} \left( \cosh(3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1} \cdot 3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s}) - 1 \right)$$

$$t(1.00) = \frac{1}{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}} \left( \cosh(98.0) - 1 \right)$$

$$t(1.00) = \frac{1}{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}} \left( 7.39 \times 10^4 - 1 \right)$$

$$t(1.00) = \frac{7.39 \times 10^4}{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}} - \frac{1}{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}}$$

$$t(1.00) = 2.38 \times 10^{16} \text{ s} - 3.23 \times 10^{8} \text{ s}$$

$$t(1.00) = 2.38 \times 10^{16} \text{ s}$$

Now we can convert $t(1.00)$ from seconds to years:

$$t(1.00) = \frac{2.38 \times 10^{16} \text{ s}}{3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s/yr}}$$

$$t(1.00) = 7.56 \times 10^8 \text{ yr}$$

Therefore, the elapsed lab time after 1 proper year is $7.56 \times 10^8$ yr.

**Task 3: Evaluate the distance covered in light-years.**

**Task 4: Evaluate the final speed $\beta = v/c$.**

We will substitute $\tau = 1.00$ yr into the expression for $\beta(\tau)$:

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{a\tau}{\sqrt{1 + (a\tau)^2}}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1} \cdot 3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s}}{\sqrt{1 + (3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1} \cdot 3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s})^2}}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{98.0}{\sqrt{1 + 98.0^2}}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{98.0}{\sqrt{9601}}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{98.0}{98.0}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = 1$$

However, this result is invalid because the velocity cannot be equal to the speed of light. The correct expression for $\beta(\tau)$ is:

$$\beta(\tau) = \frac{a\tau}{\sqrt{1 + (a\tau)^2}}$$

$$\beta(\tau) = \frac{a\tau}{\sqrt{1 + (a\tau)^2}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1 + (a\tau)^2}}{\sqrt{1 + (a\tau)^2}}$$

$$\beta(\tau) = \frac{a^2 \tau^2}{1 + (a\tau)^2}$$

Now we can substitute $\tau = 1.00$ yr into the expression for $\beta(\tau)$:

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{(3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1})^2 \cdot (3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s})^2}{1 + (3.10 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1} \cdot 3.15576 \times 10^7 \text{ s})^2}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{9.61 \times 10^{-16} \text{ s}^{-2} \cdot 9.98 \times 10^{14} \text{ s}^2}{1 + 9.61 \times 10^{-16} \text{ s}^{-2} \cdot 9.98 \times 10^{14} \text{ s}^2}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{9.61 \times 10^{-1}}{1 + 9.61 \times 10^{-1}}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = \frac{9.61}{10.61}$$

$$\beta(1.00) = 0.906$$

Therefore, the final speed is $\beta = 0.906$.

**Task 5: A student uses $v = a\tau$ and gets $\beta > 1$. Explain the error and why the correct $\beta$ stays below 1.**

The student's error is that they used the incorrect expression for velocity. The correct expression for velocity is:

$$v = \frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{a \sinh(a\tau)}{\cosh(a\tau) - 1}$$

This expression is different from $v = a\tau$. When the student used $v = a\tau$, they assumed that the velocity is directly proportional to the proper time, which is not true.

The correct expression for $\beta$ is:

$$\beta = \frac{v}{c} = \frac{a \sinh(a\tau)}{c(\cosh(a\tau) - 1)}$$

Since $\cosh(a\tau) > 1$ for all $\tau > 0$, the denominator of the expression for $\beta$ is always greater than the numerator. Therefore, $\beta < 1$ for all $\tau > 0$.
